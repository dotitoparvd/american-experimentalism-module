# Syllabus: Experimentalism and Politics in Early 20th‑Century American Music

**Course Module Duration:** 3 weeks  
**Target Audience:** Upper‑division undergraduate music history students  
**Format:** Seminar with listening, reading, and digital‑humanities components

---

## Week 1: Charles Ives and the American Transcendentalist Tradition

### Main Topic
Ives’s fusion of vernacular American music (hymn tunes, patriotic songs, ragtime) with radical polytonality, polyrhythm, and collage techniques, read through the lens of Emersonian and Thoreauvian transcendentalism.

### Required Readings
1. Ives, Charles. “Essays Before a Sonata” (excerpts).  
2. Burkholder, J. Peter. “Ives and the Nineteenth‑Century American Tradition.” *Journal of the American Musicological Society* (1985).  
3. Hitchcock, H. Wiley. *Music in the United States: A Historical Introduction* (chapter on Ives).  
4. Selected passages from Emerson, Ralph Waldo. “Self‑Reliance” and “The American Scholar.”

### Key Musical Works
- *The Unanswered Question* (1908)  
- *Three Places in New England* (1911–14)  
- *Piano Sonata No. 2, “Concord, Mass., 1840–1860”* (1911–15)  
- *Variations on “America”* (1891)

### Activities
- Close listening to *The Unanswered Question* with attention to layered tempos and keys.  
- Group discussion: How does Ives’s musical borrowing reflect a democratic, “all‑embracing” aesthetic?  
- Digital annotation exercise: Mark up a score excerpt of *Three Places in New England* using online tools.

---

## Week 2: Henry Cowell, “Tone Clusters,” and Ultramodernism

### Main Topic
Cowell’s expansion of piano technique (tone clusters, string‑plucking, fist‑and‑forearm playing), his theoretical writings on rhythm and harmony, and his role as a catalyst for the American ultramodernist movement.

### Required Readings
1. Cowell, Henry. *New Musical Resources* (excerpts).  
2. Hicks, Michael. “Henry Cowell’s ‘Rhythm‑Harmony’ and the Politics of American Modernism.” *American Music* (1993).  
3. Saylor, Eric. “Henry Cowell and the World Music Idea.” In *The Oxford Handbook of Music and World Christianities*.  
4. Nicholls, David. *American Experimental Music, 1890–1940* (chapter on Cowell).

### Key Musical Works
- *The Tides of Manaunaun* (1917)  
- *Aeolian Harp* (1923)  
- *The Banshee* (1925)  
- *Quartet Romantic* (1915–17)  
- *Piano Concerto* (1928)

### Activities
- Demonstration of tone‑cluster and string‑piano techniques via video recordings.  
- Student presentation: Compare Cowell’s “rhythm‑harmony” concepts with contemporaneous European modernist theories.  
- Listening journal entry: Describe the sonic experience of *The Banshee* and its evocation of the supernatural.

---

## Week 3: The Composers’ Collective of New York: Music, Protest, and the Labor Movement

### Main Topic
The formation, ideology, and output of the Composers’ Collective (1930s), a group of left‑wing composers who sought to create accessible, politically engaged music for workers’ choruses, rallies, and the Popular Front.

### Required Readings
1. “A Composer’s Collective Manifesto” (1934). *Modern Music*.  
2. Reuss, Richard A. “The Composers’ Collective of New York: A Historical Overview.” *American Music* (1987).  
3. Dunaway, David King. “Music and Politics in the United States: The Composers’ Collective of New York.” In *Music and Politics*.  
4. Blitzstein, Marc. “On the Use of Music in the Theater” (excerpts).

### Key Musical Works
- Marc Blitzstein – *The Cradle Will Rock* (1937)  
- Elie Siegmeister – *Strange Funeral in Braddock* (1933)  
- Charles Seeger – *The Ballad of Harry Simms* (c. 1934)  
- Ruth Crawford Seeger – *Chinaman, Laundryman* (1932)  
- Collective anthologies such as *Workers Song Book No. 1* (1934)

### Activities
- Role‑play a Collective meeting: Students propose a musical setting for a contemporary protest text.  
- Analyze the melodic and harmonic simplicity of a workers’ song versus the complex modernism of Ives and Cowell.  
- Digital mapping exercise: Plot the locations of Collective performances and labor events in 1930s New York.

---

## Assessment

- **Weekly reading responses** (300–500 words) submitted via the course blog.  
- **Collaborative digital annotation** of a primary source (group grade).  
- **Final project:** Either a traditional research paper (10–12 pages) or a digital‑humanities contribution (e.g., an expanded composer profile, a curated listening list with commentary) that extends the module’s content.

## Tools & Platforms
- GitHub repository for all course materials and student contributions.  
- Hypothesis or Genius for score/text annotation.  
- YouTube / Internet Archive for public‑domain recordings.  
- TimelineJS or StoryMapJS for spatial‑temporal visualizations.
